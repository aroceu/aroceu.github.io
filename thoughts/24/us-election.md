# What's wrong with America?

After the election results of 2024 - of which I'd been following both campaigns closely since July - I have a lot of thoughts about it all. And feelings, of course. I think we all do. No matter who you voted for, after November 6, you felt **something.**

I will say that I think it's important to remember the sources that we get our information &mdash; especially if it's bureacratic &mdash; and how much money they've made the past two days. Pair that with people's desire for immediacy and inclination to be reactionary, and the doomerism makes sense. I don't think it's helpful or right; but it's not a surprise that it's happening, and I don't blame anyone. 

But whether or not Trump keeps or loses the presidency in the coming days, if you take away anything from this article, let it be this: that no matter what it's about, if corporate interests play any part, we as common people will be exploited for their capital gain. We need to keep this in mind.

But I'll circle back to this later.

-

I'll preface this &mdash; really this time &mdash; with my own beliefs, experiences, and current sources of information.

I was born in a red state, raised in a moderate bubble in a blue one. The moderate bubble liked to believe it was liberal; but I saw about 2 black students in my high school, a white gay friend once described it as a "farmtown" in retrospect, and during the 2000 election when I was barely 6 years old, when our class ran a mock presidential poll where my classmates mostly voted based on who their parents supported, there were many more Bush "votes." The only Gore votes I remember were my one (1) Mexican classmate at the time, and myself, because I like being contrary.

Growing up I didn't realize I was in a political bubble for a long time. I didn't even know what that meant. When I was 16 my English/public speaking teacher talked about it; she was one of my favorite teachers so I was inclined to agree, but I didn't **really** know what she meant. And I'll be honest: I didn't know what she meant until this year, when I remembered everything I believed about the world as a teenager, and everything I know about the world now. They're two extremely different viewpoints: one about survival and individualism and being self-seeking, and another about community support and empathy. I like who I am now not just because I know I'm "better", but because I'm living true to my actual core beliefs as a human being. That the most important thing is community, love, and kindness; the ability to be empathetic to those who can't find it anywhere else. 

It was difficult for me to confront this, to live by these ideas, for quite some time. And I'll be honest: though this in part has to do with the moderate bubble aspect, it also has to do with my parents. They're both Chinese immigrants; the mindset they carried around as they raised me was one based on survival, keeping your head down, being acceptable, palatable, and out of harm's way. Being a coward, and willing to be disloyal - two characteristics that I also thought were just a "part" of my personality for some time.

But upon reflection, I don't think they are. I think these traits &mdash; that I feel in my bones, that **are**, in fact, a natural and physical instinct for me &mdash; are borne from my parents' focus on survival, and political views. I grew up in an affluent and safe neighborhood, so there was no real fear for survival. I grew up surrounded by people more interested in maintaining a norm than doing anything different, so there was no bedrock for any bravery. I grew up with the difficulties I faced, sure &emdash; but I grew up. And I read a lot about kids who didn't.

My core political views &mdash; once influenced by my parents &mdash; changed twice. Once was after my parents' divorce, when my mom started attending church and I, a hypersensitive people-pleasing preteen, got really sucked into the conservative beliefs of it all. Like, I made a homophobic blog post once. It was that bad. The second time was when I got into South Park yaoi.

One thing they don't tell you about moderate bubbles, especially in a swing state, is that though economically they lean right (which should tell you enough for how they tend to vote), the liberalist &mdash; not leftist &mdash; respectability politics of it all still means that if you try to, you **can** think for yourself, because on the ground with the common moderate, these people are too pussy to do anything except judge you. I'm serious: even though judging, sure, can hurt your feelings and suck, it's really not as bad as it could be. It sucks to be unsafe, but it's normal to feel uncomfortable. It's **good** to feel uncomfortable. Unfortunately, many people &mdash; moderates, libs, magahats, and yes, even progressives &mdash; can't quite tell the difference.

I bring this up not to depower the moderates &mdash; in fact, I believe that moderates like this make up a larger portion of this country than we believe. Though the county I grew up in was indeed blue this election (I checked), that doesn't mean that it was redder before. I don't know what's going on there anymore because my brain does remember the Horrors&trade; &mdash; but I do remember my first grade teacher also voting for Gore in that mock poll, two decades ago.

My main sources of political news and analysis this year have been a varying but small collective of independent left-leaning commentators who understand how the political system works (as well as the occasional news, to keep track of what most people are seeing.) They are also, incidentally, all white men (though not all straight.) This was not intentional; but I also don't believe this pattern is a negative indicator at all, if that may be your conclusion. I wanted commentary from people who want to change the system, but also understand how it works, and therefore analyze messaging from that perspective. I wanted commentary from people who could give me insight to a system that I did not have experience with, insight that I would never be privy too, as a queer woman-ish of color. Getting brunt, sometimes existential, but always based in reality analysis of politics for the last 4 months from white men who openly acknowledge not only how the system works but how they benefit from it (and don't have to worry for themselves) is my way of figuring that out, I guess.

All of these white men have different opinions on political strategy and conclusions and results, which was insightful for me to come up with my own nuanced beliefs; but they also all came from the same place, which was the acknowledgement of power that the bureacracy has, and how fucked that is when you care about people other than yourself. And even though I'm not a white man, I know that was the thing that I knew I had in common with them. My agreement is not in perfect alignment with them &emdash; with any of them, or anyone for that matter &mdash; but that doesn't mean that I won't recognize an ally when I see one.

I've also never lived in an only-blue state, though I suppose some years ago I thought at this point in time I might. I was raised in a purple state in a purple area (at the time); I went to college in a red state, then in **another** red state, and then moved to a battleground state in the south, due to everything else that's happened in my life. With how much my parents taught me about survival, that my safety is the most important thing (which it is, don't get me wrong &mdash; they just hammered it into me a **lot**), that I thought that being disloyal and cowardly was a fundamental part of my personality, you'd think that I'd have escaped to a liberal bubble now. I mean, it is an idea I'm entertaining. Eventually.

But I'm not trying to really say anything about this &mdash; it's just the way things are. I was raised around pussy moderates, I've mostly lived in politically stressful states, and my personal values are, in my opinion, very, very left of center. That's just who I am.

Last week I wrote a note on my phone of all the fundamental "beliefs" I have and how they connect to each other. I won't list them all here, but I will share the last one: I believe that fear will drive us apart, but that anger can motivate us to be kind.

What is the "left", really? What is the idea of progresivism, of change? This is not about liberalism &mdash; not about any faith in our systems. Of course, within the political (and moderate) realm there is some overlap: but we need to stop thinking about ourselves for a second. What does it **mean** to be on the left? To moderates, to the right? It means being queer, being poor, being Black or brown or Muslim or Arab. It means having blue hair and pronouns; it means transing your gender and kissing someone of the same gender as you in front of an angry homophobic picketer who wants to tell you that Jesus loves you.

The system &mdash; and ultimately the right, though many Trump voters don't realize this (I'll get to this in a second) &mdash; maintains a normalcy founded in white supremacy, in the patriarchy, in White Anglo-Saxon Protestant beliefs, of divinity and predetermined exceptionalism and bioessential superiority. The left, however (and however nuanced), is based in an idea of authenticity, of self-expression. Of personal choice and of earnestness, of uniqueness but not necessarily "individuality." Of the confidence to go against the grain, be the person who's not accepted, do the thing that's not the "proper" thing to do. It's about letting people being people, humans being humans. It's about celebrating and sustaining the varying individual experiences we have based on who we are, while still being able to recognize, care, and see each other on a human level.

When we fear each other, we isolate ourselves, and we start to hate each other. Because hate is easier than confronting our fears. We don't trust each other; we have our own personal doubts with the common people, with the good of the people. We become deterministic and say that we can't change the people in the red states, we should just annex Texas. We believe there is something fundamentally wrong with the people who don't vote in the way we want them to, not that they're ignorant, or low propensity, or have other things on their mind and no one in their lives who care enough to keep them more tapped into the news. We generalize and say that people are voting out of hatred, not fear; that people vote with malice, and not with their pockets.

Here's the thing: it sucks right now. I'm angry. You're angry. More people in the world are angry about this than not. People who are benefiting, sure, are throwing their parties in their big stupid castles. But many people across the US and around the globe &mdash; even if they may not be the entire populous half of any geographical location &mdash; are deeply disturbed, and afraid.

But that's why we need to be angry. Because we can't let our fear consume us. We can't let our anger consume us either, but anger is natural. Anger is a reaction. It's a desire for change, for justice. We see something happening and we don't like that it's happening and so we're angry about it because we want something to be done about it. We want to **do** something about it.

I think anger is a motivator, but not the solution. Not until last minute, anyway; much like how I believe that violence is a solution **only** after reason (though your mileage may vary on global policy with this one), we need to resort to anger **only** after we resort to kindness. We need to not fear one another and immediately take each other in bad faith and sow distrust from the get-go. I've talked about this before and I'll talk about this again: the tendency to be reactionary is the tendency to take people in bad faith, and you will not get anywhere with this and you will not change people's minds either, people who could be your allies. People don't like being confronted with anger, or guilt. And even if you may think they deserve to, that doesn't mean that **they** will. Some people will. But not all of them. And those who won't are, in fact, always still worth convincing.

We need to be able to be kind and build community, even with people we may fear, who may disagree with us. No one has to put up with hatred; but hatred is organized. Many people are simply ignorant; and the existence of an ignorant person is never the same danger as the existence of a hateful person. I say this as someone who grew up surrounded by these people, who was once one of these people myself. People always think they're the good guy, the main character. You don't have to roll over and agree with them on everything. But you can still treat them with humanity, and try to see eye to eye on some things, and **care about them.**

That's what it means to be kind. And that's what it means to be progressive &mdash; at least, to me and on a purely philosophical level, anyway. Being progressive also means putting in the work.

But we'll get there at some point.
